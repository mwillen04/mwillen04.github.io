School Segregation

Ever since Brown vs. Board of Education in 1954, segregating schools based soely on race has been deemed unconstitutional by the Supreme Court. Though this is true, school segregation continues to be on the rise today.


Nearing today, less black students are attending majority-white schools than from the '80's and '90's. This boasts implications that schools today are becoming more segregated then ever in the times since Brown vs. Board of Education. But why?

Wealth disparity. As wealth disparity among races increases inside of the United States, so does school segregation.
As time passes, the number of high poverty schools with a predominant minority population seem to increase while the number of low poverty schools with small minority population is decreasing.

How can we reduce school segregation?


It is possible for towns to craft boundaries differently- not focusing on sending students necessarily to the closest school- to reduce segregation. The main catalyst for modern day school segregation is wealth disparity. You can read more about wealth disparity here.

As seen by the data derived from the National Assessment of Educational Progress's (NAEP)standardized tests, black, hispanic, and indgineous people score significantly lower than their white and asian peers. This obviously presents a huge problem; the academic achievement gap is at the root of racial wealth disparity, segregation within the job market, and school segregation.

What is the cause of the academic achievement gap and what can be done to reduce it?

Wealth disparity and socioeconomic status are some of the main causes for this.
People on the lower end of the socioeconomic spectrum, which are primarily minority populations, have a clear disadvantage right from the start of their education, spiraling into the academic achievement gap. Obviously, reducing the socioeconomic gap between races would be vital in closing the academic achievement gap. There are other way to help too, such as increasing the instructional time of struggling schools and students, providing a equal and rigorous cirriculum at every single educational building in the nation, ensuring noone is left out, hiring a diverse staff of teachers that are willing to motivate and engage students of all races and ethnicities, holding professional development clinics for educators on this particular issue, and providing supplemental instruction to those who struggle. While shutting the academic achievement gap is a large feat, proper funding of all schools, especially of those in lower income communities, will be sure to move the process along.
